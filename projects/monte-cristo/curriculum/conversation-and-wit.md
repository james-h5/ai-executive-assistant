# Conversation and Wit

## Opening a Conversation
- Best openers are observational or situational, not rehearsed lines or compliments on appearance — compliments as openers signal intent immediately and put the other person on guard.
- Good default opener shape: notice something in the shared environment + a light question or comment. ("This train is somehow always exactly 4 minutes late" / "Please tell me you know if this stop is mine.")
- The goal of an opener is not to be clever — it's to be low-pressure enough that responding is easy. Wit comes later, once there's a real exchange going.

## What to Talk About
- Start light and specific, not broad and abstract — specific is easier to respond to ("what's the best thing you've eaten this week" beats "what do you like to do for fun").
- Follow genuine curiosity — ask a real follow-up on whatever they just said rather than pivoting to a prepared next topic. This is what makes conversation feel alive rather than like an interview.
- Good general territory: things currently happening (event, place, situation), light opinions ("best/worst" style questions), things they're clearly into (something they're wearing, carrying, talking about).
- Avoid interview-mode — stacking questions without offering anything of yourself. Trade information: answer your own question briefly after they answer, or share something first sometimes.

## Storytelling
- Structure: setup (where/when, brief) → the interesting/funny turn → the payoff. Cut the throat-clearing detail that doesn't serve the turn.
- Land the story and stop — don't over-explain the punchline or justify why it was funny.
- Physical stakes in this repo's projects (boxing, futures trading, backpacking Japan solo) are genuinely good story material — specific, real, not generic.

## Banter and Playfulness
- Banter = light, friendly challenge, not insult. A gentle tease ("you say that like it's a good thing") keeps things fun without risk.
- Callback humor — referencing something said a few minutes earlier — reads as attentive and clever without needing to be quick-witted in the moment.

## Listening
- The most underrated social skill. Genuine, specific follow-up questions read as more charismatic than a great opener. People remember how a conversation made them feel, not the cleverness of the lines used.
- Don't plan the next thing to say while they're still talking — actually listen, then respond to what was actually said.

## Escalating a Conversation
- Escalation = moving from surface-level exchange toward more personal, more playful, or (romantically) more direct — done gradually and by reading their response, not all at once.
- A simple escalation ladder: situational small talk → genuine curiosity/follow-up → light banter/teasing → personal question or direct statement of interest.
- If going for a number/follow-up: be direct rather than vague. "I'd like to keep talking to you, can I get your number" beats a hinting, indirect exit.

## Debrief Questions
1. What was actually said (or what would have been the natural opener if replaying it)?
2. Was there a real follow-up question available that wasn't asked?
3. Did the exchange (real or hypothetical) stay in interview-mode, or was information/opinion actually traded both ways?
