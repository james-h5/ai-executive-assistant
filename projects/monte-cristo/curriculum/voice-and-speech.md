# Voice and Speech

## Tone and Pitch
- Speak from the chest, not the throat — lower, more resonant, effortlessly reads as calm and grounded. Test: hand on chest, feel it vibrate slightly when speaking.
- Uptalk (rising pitch at the end of statements) reads as a question, undermines authority even when the words are fine. End statements flat or falling.

## Pacing
- Slower than feels natural. Rushing is a nervous-system tell — the freeze-adjacent urge to get the words out and escape the moment. A half-beat pause before responding reads as thoughtful, not slow.
- Pause instead of filling with "um," "like," "you know." Silence is not a gap that needs to be patched — a composed person is comfortable letting a beat of silence sit.

## Volume and Projection
- Project to be heard without straining — a common freeze pattern is talking quietly enough to half-hope you're not heard, which reads as uncertainty. Match the volume of the room, not below it.

## Cutting Filler Words
- The fix is not "try not to say um" — it's giving the brain permission to pause instead of filling the gap. Filler words are what happens when the mouth moves faster than the thought is ready. Slow the pace and the filler drops out naturally.

## Sounding Composed vs Rehearsed
- Composed: unhurried, willing to pause, tone stays level whether the moment is easy or has stakes.
- Rehearsed (avoid): pre-loaded lines delivered too fast, tone shifts up in pitch/speed the moment stakes appear — this is the tell that gives away nerves faster than the words themselves.

## Practical Line Delivery
- Open with an observation or a genuine question, not a compliment that sounds like a pickup line — compliments as openers put the other person on the defensive because they signal intent immediately. Read `conversation-and-wit.md` for what to actually say.
- Deliver the opener, then stop talking. Resist the urge to stack three sentences to fill space while waiting for a response.

## Debrief Questions
1. Was the pace rushed, or did it stay level throughout?
2. Any filler words — and at which point did they appear (usually right when stakes went up)?
3. Did the pitch rise at the end of statements?
