# Reading Signals

## Why This Matters
There are two failure modes, not one. The first is missing signals entirely — the freeze problem covered in `breathing-and-nerves.md`. The second is less obvious: escalating off signals that aren't really there, which reads as presumptuous rather than confident. This file is about calibration — matching the response to what's actually being signaled, no more, no less.

## The Core Rule: Match, Then Slightly Escalate
- When a signal appears, the first move is to match its level, not overreact and not freeze. Matching proves it was noticed without overcommitting.
- Once matched, a small escalation is how interest gets tested — then read the response before escalating again.
- This applies whether the signal is a smile, a laugh, a personal question, or touch.

## Verbal Signals
- Asking personal or curious questions back, laughing more than the joke earns, saying "we should do this again," keeping the conversation going past a natural exit point (a stop, the end of a round, an event winding down).
- **Response:** match the investment — ask something personal back, or extend the conversation yourself instead of waiting to be the one kept talking to.

## Proximity and Body Signals
- Closing distance, angling their body toward you, mirroring your posture, prolonged eye contact, playing with hair, leaning in.
- **Response:** mirror it back — close a little distance, hold the eye contact rather than breaking it first, lean in slightly. See `body-language.md`.

## Touch
Touch is a deliberate escalation, not an accident — a hand on the arm, shoulder, or leg during conversation is one of the clearest, highest-confidence signals someone can give before using words at all.

**The common failure is freezing or flinching.** The same nervous-system freeze from `breathing-and-nerves.md` applies here — an unexpected touch spikes the same threat response, and going rigid, pulling back, or going quiet reads as rejection even when it's just surprise.

**Response:**
1. Run the same physical reset — sigh, drop the shoulders — it works on this trigger too, not just the opening one.
2. Stay physically relaxed, hold eye contact.
3. Match at the same level — a hand back on their arm, or a light touch of your own — not more. Escalating further immediately (further touch, leaning in hard) off a single signal rushes the pace instead of reading it.
4. A verbal acknowledgment works well too: something like "that's forward — I like it," said warmly, turns an unexpected moment into a shared joke and buys a beat to recover physically without it looking like hesitation.

## Disinterest Signals
- Body angling away, arms crossing, shorter answers, checking the phone or scanning the room, eye contact breaking early and often, physical distance increasing.
- **Response:** don't escalate further. Ease off, give space, and hold the frame (`facial-expressions-and-presence.md`) — a graceful de-escalation reads far better than pushing past a clear signal. This isn't a failure to fix, it's information to act on.

## When It's Ambiguous
Not every signal is clean. When genuinely unsure, a small, low-cost escalation of your own — extending the conversation, a slightly more personal question, closing a little distance — works as a test. The response tells you more than guessing would.

## Debrief Questions
1. What was the specific signal — verbal, proximity, or touch?
2. What was the actual response in the moment — matched, over-escalated, or froze?
3. If it was touch specifically: was the freeze response present? See `breathing-and-nerves.md`.
4. Did anything afterward look like disinterest that got missed?
