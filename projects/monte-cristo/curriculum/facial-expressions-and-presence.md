# Facial Expressions and Presence

## Resting Expression
- Neutral-relaxed, not neutral-flat. A slightly softened brow and mouth reads as approachable; a flat, tense "resting" face reads as closed off or preoccupied — and makes it easy to miss when someone is signaling interest, because the face isn't scanning for it.
- Awareness starts with the face being relaxed enough to actually notice what's happening around it. A tense, inward-focused expression is part of why a moment can go unnoticed until it's passed.

## Smiling
- A genuine smile engages the eyes (slight crinkle), not just the mouth. Practicable in a mirror — the difference is very visible and very obvious to others too.
- A small, held smile in passing (train, hallway, elevator) is a complete social move on its own — it doesn't need words attached. It signals warmth and awareness without commitment either way.

## Reading a Room / Reading a Moment
- Before anything else, the job is noticing: eye contact held a beat too long, a smile, someone angled their body slightly toward you, a laugh that lands your way. These are all low-stakes signals worth acting on, not proof required before acting.
- The train moment was a clear signal (a smile directly at him) — the miss wasn't in reading it, it was in the freeze after recognizing it (see `breathing-and-nerves.md`).

## Holding Frame
- "Frame" = staying yourself regardless of the other person's response. Composure isn't dependent on the interaction going well — it's an internal state that doesn't collapse if the other person doesn't respond, is busy, or isn't interested.
- Practically: deliver the smile / opener / line, then let the outcome be whatever it is. No slumping, no visible disappointment, no over-explaining if it doesn't land. A held frame after a "miss" is more attractive and more composed than a perfect opener followed by visible deflation.

## Micro-Expressions Worth Controlling
- Eyebrow flash on greeting someone (a quick, natural raise) — universally read as friendly recognition.
- Avoid the instinctive "flinch" face (eyes widening, slight recoil) when someone unexpected engages — this is the visible version of the freeze response and can read as rejection even when it isn't intended that way.

## Debrief Questions
1. Was the signal actually noticed in the moment, or only in hindsight?
2. What was the resting face doing right before the moment (tense, distracted, relaxed)?
3. After the interaction (or non-interaction), did the frame hold, or was there visible deflation?
